We evaluate

$$
I=\int_0^\infty
\frac{\arctan x\,\ln(1+x^2)}{x(1+x^2)}\,\mathrm{d}x.
$$

The integral is convergent. Indeed, its integrand is $O(x^2)$ as $x\to0^+$ and
$O(\ln x/x^3)$ as $x\to\infty$.

## 1. Feynman's integral method

The general Leibniz integral rule is

$$
\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}
\int_{\alpha(t)}^{\beta(t)}f(x,t)\,\mathrm{d}x
=f(\beta(t),t)\beta'(t)-f(\alpha(t),t)\alpha'(t)
+\int_{\alpha(t)}^{\beta(t)}\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}(x,t)\,\mathrm{d}x.
$$

When the limits are constant, the boundary terms vanish. We obtain the special
case commonly called **Feynman's integral method**, or differentiation under the
integral sign:

$$
\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\int_A^B f(x,t)\,\mathrm{d}x
=\int_A^B\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}(x,t)\,\mathrm{d}x.
$$

This trick is particular useful when dealing with complicated integrands with $\ln f(x)$ and $\arctan f(x)$, as it transforms these terms into rational functions.

To use it here, introduce two parameters:

$$
F(a,b)=\int_0^\infty
\frac{\arctan(ax)\,\ln(1+bx^2)}{x(1+x^2)}\,\mathrm{d}x,
\qquad 0\leq a,b\leq1.
$$

Clearly,

$$
F(0,b)=F(a,0)=0,\qquad I=F(1,1).
$$

Differentiating first with respect to $a$ and then with respect to $b$ gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial^2F}{\partial b\,\partial a}(a,b)
&=\int_0^\infty
\frac{x^2}{(1+x^2)(1+a^2x^2)(1+bx^2)}\,\mathrm{d}x.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the arctangent and logarithm have disappeared, leaving a purely rational
integral. For $a,b>0$, the interchange of differentiation and integration is
justified on compact subrectangles by domination. Moreover, all the relevant
integrands are nonnegative. Applying the fundamental theorem of calculus first
on positive parameter rectangles and then using Tonelli's theorem and monotone
convergence as their lower edges tend to zero yields

$$
I=\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{\partial^2F}{\partial b\,\partial a}(a,b)
\,\mathrm{d}b\,\mathrm{d}a.
$$

Before using complex analysis, make the reciprocal substitution

$$
x=\frac1t,\qquad \mathrm{d}x=-\frac{1}{t^2}\,\mathrm{d}t.
$$

The limits $x=0$ and $x=\infty$ become $t=\infty$ and $t=0$,
respectively. Hence

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial^2F}{\partial b\,\partial a}(a,b)
&=\int_\infty^0
\frac{t^{-2}}{(1+t^{-2})(1+a^2t^{-2})(1+bt^{-2})}
\left(-\frac{1}{t^2}\right)\,\mathrm{d}t\\
&=\int_0^\infty
\frac{t^2}{(t^2+1)(t^2+a^2)(t^2+b)}\,\mathrm{d}t.
\end{aligned}
$$

This puts the rational function into the standard form needed for a
semicircular-contour calculation.

## 2. The rational integral by residues

We first prove the useful identity

$$
\int_0^\infty
\frac{x^2\,\mathrm{d}x}
{(x^2+p^2)(x^2+q^2)(x^2+r^2)}
=\frac{\pi}{2(p+q)(q+r)(r+p)},
\qquad p,q,r>0.
$$

Consider

$$
g(z)=\frac{z^2}{(z^2+p^2)(z^2+q^2)(z^2+r^2)}
$$

on the positively oriented upper-half-plane semicircular contour $C_R$. Recall
the Residue Theorem: if $h$ is holomorphic on and inside a positively oriented
simple closed contour $C$, except at isolated singularities $z_1,\ldots,z_n$,
then

$$
\oint_C h(z)\,\mathrm{d}z
=2\pi i\sum_{k=1}^n\operatorname{Res}(h,z_k).
$$

Assume first that $p,q,r$ are positive and pairwise distinct. Factoring the
denominator gives

$$
g(z)=\frac{z^2}
{(z-ip)(z+ip)(z-iq)(z+iq)(z-ir)(z+ir)}.
$$

The six poles are therefore $\pm ip$, $\pm iq$, and $\pm ir$. Every
corresponding linear factor has multiplicity one, so all six are **simple
poles**, or poles of order one. The upper semicircle encloses exactly $ip$, $iq$,
and $ir$.

More generally, if $z_0$ is an isolated singularity of a function $h$, then in
some punctured neighborhood of $z_0$ the function has a Laurent expansion

$$
h(z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_n(z-z_0)^n.
$$

The residue of $h$ at $z_0$ is, by definition, the coefficient of
$(z-z_0)^{-1}$:

$$
\operatorname{Res}(h,z_0)=c_{-1}.
$$

If $z_0$ is a pole of order $m$, the residue can be calculated without writing
the entire Laurent series by using the general formula

$$
\operatorname{Res}(h,z_0)
=\frac{1}{(m-1)!}
\lim_{z\to z_0}
\frac{\mathrm{d}^{m-1}}{\mathrm{d}z^{m-1}}
\left[(z-z_0)^m h(z)\right].
$$

For $m=1$, no differentiation is required, and this reduces to the simple-pole
formula

$$
\operatorname{Res}(g,z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0}(z-z_0)g(z).
$$

At $z=ip$, cancelling $z-ip$ and then substituting $z=ip$ gives

$$
\operatorname{Res}(g,ip)
=\lim_{z\to ip}
\frac{z^2}{(z+ip)(z^2+q^2)(z^2+r^2)}=\frac{-p^2}{2ip(q^2-p^2)(r^2-p^2)}=\frac{ip}{2(q^2-p^2)(r^2-p^2)}.
$$

Similarly, the substitutions at $iq$ and $ir$ give

$$
\operatorname{Res}(g,iq)
=\lim_{z\to iq}
\frac{z^2}{(z+iq)(z^2+p^2)(z^2+r^2)}=\frac{(iq)^2}{2iq(p^2-q^2)(r^2-q^2)}=\frac{iq}{2(p^2-q^2)(r^2-q^2)},
$$

$$
\operatorname{Res}(g,ir)
=\lim_{z\to ir}
\frac{z^2}{(z+ir)(z^2+p^2)(z^2+q^2)}=\frac{(ir)^2}{2ir(p^2-r^2)(q^2-r^2)}=\frac{ir}{2(p^2-r^2)(q^2-r^2)}.
$$

Since $g(z)=O(|z|^{-4})$, the integral over the large semicircle tends to zero
as $R\to\infty$. The Residue Theorem now gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}g(x)\,\mathrm{d}x
&=2\pi i\left[
\operatorname{Res}(g,ip)+\operatorname{Res}(g,iq)
+\operatorname{Res}(g,ir)
\right]\\
&=-\pi\left[
\frac{p}{(q^2-p^2)(r^2-p^2)}
+\frac{q}{(p^2-q^2)(r^2-q^2)}
+\frac{r}{(p^2-r^2)(q^2-r^2)}
\right]\\
&=\frac{\pi}{(p+q)(q+r)(r+p)}.
\end{aligned}
$$

In the last step, the three rational terms were put over a common denominator
and factored. Because $g$ is even, division by $2$ proves the stated identity.
Cases in which two or three parameters coincide follow by continuity; in those
cases the complex function itself has higher-order poles, but the limiting
integral formula remains valid.

Now put $s=\sqrt b$. The rational integral obtained after the reciprocal
substitution has the preceding form with $p=1$, $q=a$, and $r=s$. Consequently,

$$
\frac{\partial^2F}{\partial b\,\partial a}(a,b)=\frac{\pi}{2(1+a)(a+\sqrt b)(1+\sqrt b)}.
$$

## 3. Recovering the original integral as a double integral

Substituting the last result and then setting $b=s^2$, so that
$\mathrm{d}b=2s\,\mathrm{d}s$, we obtain

$$
I
=\frac{\pi}{2}\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{\mathrm{d}b\,\mathrm{d}a}
{(1+a)(a+\sqrt b)(1+\sqrt b)}=\pi\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{s}{(1+a)(a+s)(1+s)}\,\mathrm{d}s\,\mathrm{d}a.
$$

Denote the last double integral without the factor $\pi$ by $D$. The square
$[0,1]^2$ is symmetric under $a\leftrightarrow s$. Hence

$$
D=\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{a}{(1+a)(a+s)(1+s)}\,\mathrm{d}s\,\mathrm{d}a.
$$

Adding this expression to the original one for $D$ gives

$$
2D
=\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{a+s}{(1+a)(a+s)(1+s)}\,\mathrm{d}s\,\mathrm{d}a
=\int_0^1\int_0^1
\frac{1}{(1+a)(1+s)}\,\mathrm{d}s\,\mathrm{d}a
=\left(\int_0^1\frac{\mathrm{d}u}{1+u}\right)^2
=\ln^2 2.
$$

Finally, we get

$$
\int_0^\infty
\frac{\arctan x\,\ln(1+x^2)}{x(1+x^2)}\,\mathrm{d}x
=\frac{\pi}{2}\ln^2 2.
$$
