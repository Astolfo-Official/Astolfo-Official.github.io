On the curve $L:\ y=\sin x,\ x \in [0,\pi]$, following formulas are useful.

$$
\int_0^{\pi}x f(\sin x)\,\mathrm{d}x=\frac{\pi}{2}\int_0^{\pi}f(\sin x)\,\mathrm{d}x
$$

We firstly expend the line integal as

$$
\int_Lx\,\mathrm{d}s=\int_0^{\pi}x\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}\,\mathrm{d}x
=\frac{\pi}{2}\int_0^{\pi}\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}\,\mathrm{d}x
=\pi\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}\,\mathrm{d}x.
$$

Let $t=\frac{\pi}{2}-x$, then

$$
\int_{\frac{\pi}{4}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}\,\mathrm{d}x
=\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{4}}\sqrt{1+\sin^2 x}\,\mathrm{d}x.
$$

Hence

$$
\int_Lx\,\mathrm{d}s=\pi\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{4}}\left(\sqrt{1+\sin^2 x}+\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}\right)\,\mathrm{d}x.
$$

Consider the function

$$
f(x)=\sqrt{1+\sin^2 x}+\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x},\quad x\in\left[0,\frac{\pi}{4}\right].
$$

$$
f'(x)=\frac{2\sin x\cos x}{2\sqrt{1+\sin^2 x}}-\frac{2\sin x\cos x}{2\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}}
=\frac{1}{2}\sin 2x\cdot \frac{\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}-\sqrt{1+\sin^2 x}}{\sqrt{1+\sin^2 x}\sqrt{1+\cos^2 x}}>0.
$$

Hence $f(x)$ is strictly increasing on $\left[0,\frac{\pi}{4}\right]$, so

$$
\sqrt{2}+1=f(0)<f(x)<f\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)=\sqrt{6},\quad x\in\left(0,\frac{\pi}{4}\right).
$$

Finally, we get the bound of the line integal

$$
\frac{1+\sqrt{2}}{4}\pi^2<\int_Lx\,\mathrm{d}s<\frac{\sqrt{6}}{4}\pi^2.
$$
